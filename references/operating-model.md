# Operating Model

Use this file for function design, governance, intake, and scalable execution.

## Design Components

Define:

1. Mission: why the function exists
2. Scope: what it owns, shapes, supports, and does not own
3. Stakeholders: leaders, managers, RevOps, marketing, product, customer teams
4. Service model: strategic programs, ongoing support, reactive requests, embedded partnership
5. Intake and prioritization: how requests enter, get triaged, and are accepted or rejected
6. Operating cadence: planning, review, reinforcement, reporting
7. Success measures: outputs, adoption, performance levers, business outcomes

## Design Principles

- Avoid becoming a catch-all service desk.
- Clarify whether enablement owns execution, advises, or co-owns with another function.
- Build around business priorities, not content requests.
- Standardize intake to protect strategic focus.
- Match staffing and specialization to maturity and scale.
- Codify strategy in a charter with definition, vision, mission, objectives, scope, and roadmap.
- Include managers explicitly in audience scope when adoption depends on inspection and reinforcement.

## Common Operating Models

- Centralized enablement: strong consistency and governance, risk of distance from field reality
- Embedded or federated enablement: strong context, risk of inconsistency and duplication
- Hybrid model: central standards with embedded execution

## Scaling Heuristics

- Early-stage and relatively uniform motions favor centralized designs.
- Rising localization, segment complexity, or product variation favors hybrid designs.
- Decentralized models require unusually strong local leadership and will otherwise weaken consistency.
- Complexity should drive structure at least as much as headcount.

## Charter Components

Use a durable charter that defines:

1. definition of enablement in the company
2. vision
3. mission
4. audience scope
5. objectives and KPIs
6. roadmap and sequencing
7. ownership boundaries

## Priority Filters

Approve initiatives that meet several of these conditions:

- tied to a strategic revenue objective
- affect a broad or high-value segment
- have clear stakeholders and owners
- are measurable
- have manager and leader sponsorship
- are feasible with current capacity

## Intake Questions

- What business problem is this meant to solve?
- Which audience is affected?
- What evidence shows the problem exists?
- What happens if nothing changes?
- Who owns implementation?
- What metric will move if this works?
- What tradeoff is being accepted by prioritizing this now?

## Swimlane Logic

Clarify vertical and horizontal ownership:

- inside enablement: standards, methodology, onboarding architecture, platform ownership, content governance, analytics, intake
- with sales managers: frontline coaching, 1:1s, deal reviews, pipeline reviews
- with PMM and Product: positioning, launches, messaging inputs
- with RevOps: process design, workflows, automations, data infrastructure

Unclear swimlanes are an operating-model flaw, not a communication issue.
